Why Acharya exists

The hallucination problem, in the country least equipped to catch it

The problem with single-LLM tutors

  • One model, one voice, one chance to be wrong — confidently.
  • A Class 7 child cannot fact-check a chatbot. Neither can their parent.
  • Most Indian school children study in vernacular languages where models are weaker and hallucinations are more frequent.
  • "AI-generated, may be inaccurate" disclaimers don't fix the lesson.

What Acharya does differently

  • Every question runs through three independent LLMs in parallel.
  • A fourth judge model scores how much each model's claims agree with the others'.
  • Brooks–Iyengar fault-tolerant fusion discards outliers and picks the answer that the surviving consensus supports.
  • Every reply ships with a verdict — Verified, Likely correct, or Needs human review — so parents see exactly how confident the system is.

How it works

One question. Three witnesses. One verifiable answer.

A · Subject vote≥ 2 of 3 must agree on the subject, else flag for review.
B · Fact scoringJudge scores every fact 0–2 against the other two models' claims.
C · FusionDiscard outliers; pick highest-confidence model whose facts survive.
D · VerdictThreshold rules assign Verified, Likely correct, or Needs review.

Every answer ships with a verdict

The parent never has to guess how confident the AI is.

✓
Verified
3 / 3 subject agree · ≥ 80% facts agree

All three models converged on the same subject and most of the underlying facts cross-validated. Safe to teach.

⚠
Likely correct
2 / 3 subject agree · ≥ 60% facts agree

A majority converged but at least one model disagreed. Answer is delivered with a soft caveat in the bubble.

✕
Needs human review
below either threshold

Disagreement is too sharp to trust. Surfaced to the parent dashboard for follow-up — not delivered as fact.

The pedigree

Why Brooks–Iyengar?

The Brooks–Iyengar algorithm, introduced by Dr. S. S. Iyengar and Richard Brooks, was originally a fault-tolerant consensus protocol for distributed sensor networks — fusing readings from multiple sensors when any minority of them might be faulty.

We treat each LLM as a sensor. Each one observes the same input — the student's question — and emits a noisy, occasionally faulty reading. The Brooks–Iyengar fusion step is structurally identical: tolerate a minority of faulty signals, fuse the rest into a single defensible output.

"The product is, structurally, a textbook application of the algorithm to LLM outputs treated as faulty sensors — the agreement panel is the same kind of graph distributed-systems researchers have been reading for two decades."
