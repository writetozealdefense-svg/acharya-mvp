A Acharya · PRD v1.0 1. Executive summary

Section 1

Executive summary

Acharya is a WhatsApp-native AI tutor for Indian school children (Class 6–10) that responds in vernacular languages and verifies every answer across three independent LLMs before sending it. The verification engine is a direct application of the Brooks–Iyengar fault-tolerant consensus algorithm — treating each LLM as a noisy, occasionally faulty sensor, and fusing their outputs in a way that tolerates a minority of bad signals.

The product targets the largest under-served education population on Earth: roughly 250 million Indian children in school, the majority of whom study in a vernacular language and cannot fact-check a chatbot. Single-LLM tutors will hallucinate confidently in those languages, and neither the child nor the parent has a way to know. Acharya makes verification a structural property of the product, not a disclaimer.

What is built today

  • End-to-end MVP demo on Next.js 14 with a WhatsApp-look chat at localhost:3000.
  • Real Brooks–Iyengar consensus across Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-4o-mini, and Gemini 2.0 Flash, calling all three in parallel and using a fourth Claude call as the fact-agreement judge.
  • Claude Sonnet vision for image-to-question extraction (no client-side OCR).
  • Curriculum-grounded retrieval over 16 textbook excerpts in Kannada, Hindi, and English.
  • Three pre-loaded demo scenarios including a hallucination-catch scenario showing what happens when models disagree.
  • Parent dashboard with subject breakdown, concept tracker, and recent-questions log.

What unlocks scale

  • WhatsApp Business API onboarding via a Meta BSP (AiSensy, Gupshup, or similar).
  • Voice-note intake and reply, matching the rural-India usage pattern that already exists.
  • Curriculum coverage expanded from a 16-excerpt seed corpus to full Class 6–10 across CBSE, NCERT, and the major State Boards.
  • Family-account semantics (one parent, multiple children, per-child progress tracking).

The single most important thing to remember about Acharya

Every other AI tutor on the market today ships answers wrapped in “AI‐generated, may be inaccurate.” Acharya is the only one that ships with a verdict — Verified Likely correct or Needs human review — and an audit trail of why.

A Acharya · PRD v1.0 3. The opportunity

Section 3

The opportunity

Market sizing

India has approximately 250 million school children in Class 1–12 — the world's largest school-going cohort. Of these, roughly 120 million are in Class 6–10, the age band where homework help becomes the dominant educational ask outside the classroom. A super-majority study in a vernacular medium of instruction (Hindi, Kannada, Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Bengali, etc.). The premium English-medium private-tutor market is well-served; the vernacular medium Class 6–10 market is not.

Why now

  • WhatsApp penetration. 500M+ Indian users; effectively the primary compute-and-communication surface for the family unit. No app to install, no UX to teach.
  • Frontier LLMs are now multilingual. Claude, GPT, and Gemini all produce defensible Class 6–10 answers in Hindi and Kannada today — defensible most of the time. The remaining tail of hallucinations is the problem Acharya solves.
  • Per-question economics work. Three LLM calls + one judge call costs in the order of cents per question at current pricing. Sustainable at parent-pays prices well below an in-person tutor.

The problem with what exists today

Single-LLM tutors

One model, one voice, one chance to be wrong. The child has no way to detect confident-sounding hallucinations — especially in a vernacular language where model accuracy is meaningfully lower than English.

Edtech apps

Require app installation, English UI, and a paid subscription. Effectively gated to urban English-medium families. Vernacular-medium parents in tier 2–4 cities are excluded from the design.

WhatsApp tutor groups

Manual, slow, and dependent on the goodwill of a teacher who is already overloaded. No verifiability, no audit trail, no progress tracking.

Generic ChatGPT-style apps

Not designed for children. No curriculum grounding. No parent visibility into what the child is asking or what verdict the AI gave. Disclaimers in legalese, not in actionable form.

Acharya's wedge

A WhatsApp-native vernacular tutor whose verification — not its answers — is the differentiator. The Brooks–Iyengar consensus engine is a defensible academic foundation that is also a memorable product story.

A Acharya · PRD v1.0 4. Product vision & principles

Section 4

Product vision & principles

Vision statement

Every Indian school child should have access to a tutor that speaks their language, sees their textbook, and admits when it might be wrong. Acharya is that tutor, on the one app every Indian family already uses.

Operating principles

1. Verification is structural, not cosmetic.

Every answer carries a verdict computed from real model agreement, not a static disclaimer. The verdict is visible in the chat bubble — not buried in settings.

2. Vernacular-first, English-second.

The product detects the child's language from the first message and replies in kind. Switching languages is a one-tap pill, not a settings menu.

3. The intake is whatever the child is comfortable with.

Type, snap a textbook page, or send a voice note. Acharya does the work of converting these into a question, not the child.

4. The parent is a first-class user.

The parent dashboard is a real product surface, not a marketing afterthought. Parents see what their child is asking, which subjects are getting Verified answers, and which topics are being flagged for review.

5. Honest failure beats confident hallucination.

If the consensus engine cannot reach a Verified or Likely correct verdict, the child sees a Needs review state and the question goes to the parent dashboard for follow-up. We do not paper over disagreement.

6. Built on giants' shoulders.

The product is a thin, opinionated application of well-established work — Brooks–Iyengar consensus, frontier LLMs, WhatsApp's distribution. We add taste and judgment, not new model research.

Explicit non-goals (v1.0)

  • We are not building our own LLM, our own messaging app, or our own curriculum.
  • We are not targeting Class 11–12 board prep or competitive-exam tutoring in v1.
  • We are not building a parent paywall or per-question billing in v1; pricing is TBD and gated on retention data.
  • We are not localising into the Devanagari numeral system unless user research shows a strong preference; we use Hindu‐Arabic numerals which the textbooks use.
  • We are not adding a teacher-facing dashboard in v1.

A Acharya · PRD v1.0 5. Target users & personas

Section 5

Target users & personas

Primary persona — Aanya, Class 7, Hubballi

Profile. 12 years old, studies in a Karnataka State Board school, medium of instruction is Kannada. English is a second-language subject she finds difficult. She has access to her father's smartphone for an hour after dinner most weekdays.

Goal. Get help with that day's homework before her father loses patience or her mother needs the phone.

Trigger. A specific homework problem she does not understand.

Failure mode without Acharya. She copies an answer from a friend's notebook, or skips the question, or asks her father who is not a subject teacher. Conceptual gaps compound silently.

What success looks like. She types or photographs a question, receives a Kannada explanation in 8–10 seconds with a Verified badge, follows up with one or two clarifying questions, finishes the problem in under 4 minutes, and goes to bed.

Secondary persona — Vinod, Aanya's father

Profile. 42 years old, accountant, active WhatsApp user, completed Class 12 himself but has not done Class 7 science in 25 years. Has two children.

Goal. Know what his daughter is studying and whether she's understanding it — without having to learn the material himself.

What success looks like. Once a week he opens the parent dashboard, sees a green dot next to "Photosynthesis" and "Linear equations", an amber dot next to "Acids and bases", and a one-line summary like "12 questions this week, 9 Verified, 3 Likely correct, 0 review needed." He tells his daughter she's doing well in math and asks her to slow down on chemistry.

Tertiary persona — the school teacher (out-of-scope for v1)

Teachers are an obvious adjacent persona — they could use the same engine to prepare lessons, generate practice problems, and review classroom-wide question patterns. Explicitly out-of-scope for v1 to keep the surface area focused. Phase 3 on the roadmap.

Section 6

Solution overview

The end-to-end loop, from a child's question to a verified answer, is shown below. It runs in roughly 7 seconds today against live model APIs. Every box on the diagram is a real piece of code in the MVP repository.

Figure 1. The Acharya consensus loop. The student question fans out to three independent LLMs in parallel, then a Claude judge call scores fact-by-fact agreement, then Brooks–Iyengar fusion picks the answer from the highest-confidence eligible model and assigns one of three verdicts based on threshold rules.

Why three models, not two or five

Two would be a tie when they disagree. Five would push per-question latency and cost past usable. Three is the smallest number that admits a meaningful majority vote — and three is exactly what Brooks–Iyengar fault-tolerant consensus assumes when tolerating a single faulty sensor (the canonical 3f+1 setup with f=1 fault).

Why three different vendors

Independence of the votes is everything. Three calls to Claude would correlate. Three calls to OpenAI would correlate. Pulling Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google means three different training corpora, three different fine-tuning regimes, and three different RLHF policies — the closest you can get to true sensor independence in this domain.

A Acharya · PRD v1.0 8. The Brooks–Iyengar consensus algorithm

Section 8

The Brooks–Iyengar consensus algorithm

The original Brooks–Iyengar algorithm (Brooks & Iyengar, 1996) was a fault-tolerant data fusion procedure for distributed sensor networks. Given N sensors of which up to f may be Byzantine-faulty, it produces a single fused output whose accuracy is bounded even in the presence of arbitrary failures, by discarding outliers and weighing the remaining sensor outputs by overlap.

Acharya treats each LLM as a sensor. The input — a tutoring question — is the same physical phenomenon being observed. Each LLM returns a noisy, occasionally faulty observation: an answer plus a list of factual claims it depends on. The fusion problem becomes: combine three observations into one trustworthy answer in a way that is robust to one model being wrong.

The four-step pipeline

Step A — Subject vote

Each model classifies the question into one of eight subjects (physics, chemistry, biology, math, history, geography, language, other). At least 2 of 3 must agree on the subject; if they don't, the question is flagged for human review immediately and no answer is delivered.

Step B — Fact-agreement scoring

Each model emits 3–6 atomic factual claims supporting its answer, in English so they can be compared cross-language. A fourth Claude call serves as the judge: for every claim made by every model, the judge inspects whether the other two models' claim lists contain agreeing assertions (semantic match, not string match). This yields a score in {0, 1, 2} per claim — the number of other models that agreed.

Step C — Fault-tolerant fusion

Per-model statistics are computed: total facts, surviving facts (score ≥1), average score. Eligible models are those whose subject matches the majority subject, whose surviving fact set is non-empty, and whose average score is ≥1. Among eligible models, the highest-confidence one is chosen as the answer source. If no model is eligible, the engine falls back to the highest-confidence ok-model and the verdict is forced to Needs review.

Step D — Verdict assignment

VerdictThreshold
Verified3 of 3 models agreed on subject AND ≥80% of facts have score ≥1 AND eligible set is non-empty.
Likely correct2 of 3 models agreed on subject AND ≥60% of facts have score ≥1 AND eligible set is non-empty.
Needs human reviewBelow either of the above thresholds, OR the eligible set is empty.

Why this maps cleanly to the original algorithm

Brooks–Iyengar's contribution was the geometric overlap step — sensors whose intervals overlap with at least N−f others contribute to the fused output, others are discarded. Our fact-agreement scoring is the LLM analogue: claims are intervals in semantic space, and the judge measures pairwise overlap. The "discard outliers, fuse the rest" operation is the same operation, lifted from real-valued sensor readings to natural-language claims.

Section 9

Technical architecture

MVP stack (today)

  • Frontend & API: Next.js 14 App Router with TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, React 18.
  • Model SDKs: @anthropic-ai/sdk, openai, @google/generative-ai.
  • Image rendering: Claude Sonnet vision (no client-side OCR, no Tesseract).
  • Math: react-markdown + remark-math + rehype-katex.
  • State: React useState + localStorage. No database in the demo.
  • Icons: lucide-react.

Production stack (target)

  • Messaging: WhatsApp Business API via a Meta-approved BSP (AiSensy or Gupshup, TBD).
  • Webhook handler: Same Next.js API routes deployed on Vercel or AWS.
  • Persistence: Postgres (Neon or Supabase) for accounts, conversations, verdicts. Object storage (S3 or R2) for media.
  • Vector store (FR-4.5): pgvector or Pinecone, only when curriculum corpus exceeds ~500 documents.
  • Observability: Per-call latency, cost, verdict distribution, model failure rates. Tooling TBD; native Vercel + a thin custom dashboard initially.

Per-question latency budget

Today the end-to-end loop is roughly 7 seconds against live APIs. The breakdown: ~2.5s for the slowest of three parallel model calls, ~3s for the judge call, ~0.5s for retrieval and orchestration, ~1s for network. Headroom is the judge call — future work batches all judge queries into a single call to cut ~2s.

Section 10

UX flows

Flow 1 — Type-and-ask (the 80% case)

  1. Child opens WhatsApp, taps Acharya, types a question in their language.
  2. Typing dots appear in the assistant bubble (~7s).
  3. Answer arrives with a green Verified badge in the bubble corner.
  4. If the child wants the reasoning, they tap "Show consensus reasoning" in the chat header to expand a side panel showing all three model votes (in the local demo). On WhatsApp itself, this becomes a one-line link "Why this answer?" that opens a webview.

Flow 2 — Snap-the-page

  1. Child taps the paperclip, picks a photo of the textbook page.
  2. Image preview appears with "Reading question..." caption.
  3. Within 2–3s, the extracted question text is auto-populated in the input field with the image preserved as an attachment.
  4. Child taps Send (or edits the extracted text first if needed).
  5. Same answer flow as Flow 1, with the image surfaced in the user message bubble.

Flow 3 — Disagreement

  1. Question is sent normally.
  2. Consensus engine runs; verdict comes back as Likely correct or Needs human review.
  3. Answer is delivered with the appropriate badge. For Likely correct, a soft caveat is appended ("Two of three models agreed on this; check the panel for the third opinion.").
  4. For Needs review, no answer text is delivered; a message says "Acharya isn't confident enough to teach this answer. Your parent will see this in the dashboard." and the question goes to the parent dashboard's review queue.

Flow 4 — Parent dashboard

  1. Parent opens the dashboard from the chat header menu (or a weekly WhatsApp link, in production).
  2. Top of page: 4 summary cards, then the subject donut chart with legend.
  3. Below: concept tracker with green/amber/red dots per topic.
  4. Below: last 20 questions chronologically with their verdict icons, subject, language, and timestamp.
  5. Tapping a Needs-review item opens the full question and the consensus panel for that exchange (P1).

A Acharya · PRD v1.0 11. Privacy, security, & data handling

Section 11

Privacy, security, & data handling

Children's data: the operating posture

Acharya's primary users are minors. The product's data posture must clear that bar before anything else.

Commitments (v1.0)

  • No training on child data. Conversations are not used to fine-tune any model, nor sent to model providers under contracts that allow training. We use the Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google API endpoints whose default policy is no-training, and we do not opt into training programs.
  • No resale. Conversation data is never sold, shared with advertisers, or used to build profiles for third parties.
  • Minimum retention. The default retention is 90 days for the parent dashboard's recent-questions feature, then automatic purge. Verdicts and subject labels (without question text) may be retained longer for product analytics.
  • Parent visibility. The parent always has access to the same data the system has — the dashboard is the primary interface for that visibility.
  • Indian data residency (target). Production storage in an India-resident region of the chosen cloud provider, before public launch.

What we still need to do

  • Privacy policy review by an Indian counsel before public launch.
  • WhatsApp Business profile compliance with Meta's child-product rules.
  • Decide opt-in vs opt-out semantics for the parent's weekly summary push.
  • Decide whether voice notes are stored at all once transcribed.

Security baseline (v1.0)

  • HTTPS only. Webhook signature verification for Meta callbacks.
  • API keys in environment variables, never in source control. The MVP repo's .gitignore blocks .env.local at the root.
  • Rate-limiting per phone number on the WhatsApp webhook to prevent abuse.
  • Standard Postgres-side encryption at rest from the chosen managed provider.

Section 12

Success metrics

Engagement (per child)

  • Daily active rate among onboarded children (target: ≥40% by month 3 post-launch).
  • Median questions per active day (target: 3–5).
  • Median session length (target: 4–7 minutes — long enough to be useful, short enough to not be dependency).

Quality (per question)

  • Distribution of verdicts: target ≥75% Verified, ≤5% Needs review.
  • End-to-end latency p50 / p95 (target: ≤7s / ≤12s).
  • Per-model failure rate (target: ≤2% per provider).

Trust (per parent)

  • Weekly dashboard open rate among parents (target: ≥30%).
  • Review-queue resolution rate (target: ≥60% of Needs-review items reviewed within 7 days).

Cost

  • Average per-question cost (target: ≤ ₹1.5 at scale, including the judge call).
